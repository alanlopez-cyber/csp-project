01 · Background
Why Valencia & the Mediterranean?

Valencia, on the western Mediterranean coast, has recorded increasing August temperatures exceeding 34°C over the past two decades. Simultaneously, EEA records show rising Mediterranean Sea Surface Temperatures (SST).

The Mediterranean is warming 20% faster than the global ocean average (IPCC AR6), making it a critical early-warning system for global ocean health.

Marine ecosystems here — especially Posidonia oceanica seagrass meadows — are highly sensitive to temperature increase and provide oxygen, carbon sequestration, and fish nursery habitat.

Hypothesis Years with higher August air temperatures would produce more heatwave days, and this would correlate positively with higher Mediterranean SST anomalies in the same year.
02 · Methodology
Scientific Approach
1
Data extraction — EEA Mediterranean SST (Mediterranean Sea column, Yearly means) parsed via Python csv.DictReader
2
Air temp filter — RVVCCA daily records filtered to August only; max, mean, and heatwave days (≥30°C threshold) calculated per year
3
Overlap analysis — 11-year window 2004–2014 where both datasets share records used for correlation
4
Decade means — SST and air temp grouped by decade using defaultdict for long-term trend analysis
5
Pearson r — Three correlation coefficients computed: max air × SST, avg air × SST, heatwave days × SST
03 · Limitations
Data Constraints
SAMPLE Only 11 years of overlap between datasets — too small for statistically robust conclusions
TEMPORAL SST is an annual average; air temp is August-only — comparing different time resolutions introduces systematic mismatch
RECENCY EEA SST dataset ends 2014 — misses post-2015 extreme heatwave events that might show different patterns
ACCESS Monthly Valencia-specific SST data not publicly available; forces use of basin-wide annual average

07 · Conclusion
Hypothesis Not Supported — But Warming is Real

Local August air temperature spikes and heatwave days in Valencia show no meaningful positive correlation with Mediterranean SST anomalies in the same year. The two processes appear to be independent on an annual timescale.

The hottest air year (2010 · 33.8°C) corresponded to only a moderate SST anomaly (+0.34°C) and just 2 heatwave days. The highest SST year (2014 · +0.65°C) recorded a modest air max of 29.9°C and zero heatwave days — the clearest evidence of divergence.

Yet the long-term SST trend is undeniable: +0.94°C from 1870s to 2010s. Mediterranean SST warming is a slow, accumulative process driven by global ocean heat uptake — not directly triggered by individual summer heatwaves.

09 · Further Implications
Why This Still Matters
🌿
Posidonia oceanica at risk Mediterranean seagrass meadows suffer die-off above 28°C sustained over weeks. SST at +0.65°C already approaches critical thresholds independent of air heatwaves.
🐟
Fish population cascades Marine plants support fish nurseries. Rising baseline SST — even without heatwave coupling — threatens the food web that sustains Valencia's fishing economy.
🌍
Mediterranean as early warning Warming 20% faster than global average means what happens here forecasts future global ocean states. Independent air-sea warming is itself an alarming finding.
📊
Methodology can be replicated The same Python pipeline applied to monthly SST data with monthly air temperature could produce a more conclusive correlation analysis for any coastal city.
